SCOPE:  This SOP is applicable to all Comparative Medicine personnel involved in the sentinel rodent health surveillance program.

SOP OWNER:   Attending Veterinarian
PURPOSE:  To define the microbial status of rodent colonies and outline the proper procedures for specimen collection and examination if applicable
LOCATION:  All Facilities

  1. Responsibilities

    1. Animal Care Technician (ACT)

      1. Adhere to procedures as outlined in this SOP.

      2. Document all activities as outlined below in Recordkeeping.

      3. Inform veterinarian about any health concerns with the sentinel animals.

    2. Coordinator, Administrative Services (CAS)

      1. Train CM staff on this SOP regularly but at least once a year. Newly hired personnel will be trained during the initial training period.

      2. Train CM staff on appropriate procedures necessary to perform health evaluations, necropsies, tissue collections and relevant diagnostic procedures.

    3. Facilities Manager (FM)

      1. Oversees receipt of animals into quarantine housing, initial health evaluation, care/husbandry procedures, pre-placement and final collection of test samples, shipping to commercial laboratory and in- house testing as directed by AV and outlined in this SOP.

      2. Inform AV immediately about any equivocal or positive results and abnormal findings and/or observations.

      3. Assures that responsible Animal Care Technicians are appropriately trained and implement procedures as delineated in this SOP.

    4. Veterinarian/Director

      1. Adhere to procedures as outlined in this SOP.

      2. Ensure procedures are followed as outlined in this SOP.

      3. Ensure appropriate training is provided to particular personnel performing this function.

      4. Review all health records and test results if applicable.

      5. Updates rodent health surveillance program as new knowledge, information and diagnostic procedures become available.

  2. Safety

    1. Eating, drinking, chewing gum, and applying cosmetics is not permitted within the facility.

    2. Appropriate PPE has to be worn and universal precautions followed when working with rodents.

    3. Wash hands after handling animals and specimen for examination.

    4. Mice are capable of inflicting scratches and bites. Use caution and proper handling and restraint techniques.

    5. In the event of a bite or a scratch, notify Supervisor immediately and complete injury report. All positive results of in-house tests have to be confirmed by the veterinarian.

    6. Test results received from Charles River have to be reviewed by the Attending Veterinarian and appropriate follow up steps implemented if results are positive for tested pathogens.

  3. Procedure

    1. General:

      1. Surveillance is conducted for subclinical and clinical diseases that could jeopardize the validity and reproducibility of research data, or complicate its interpretation, or cause zoonotic concerns.

      2. The SOP outlines the proper procedures for exposing sentinel rodents or contact animals to soiled bedding or colony animals, respectively as well as testing colony animals directly (see also FAUSOP 200 Cage Changing).

      3. All mouse and rat colonies are monitored according to this SOP. Rodent populations for acute studies, i.e. less than 6 weeks housed in CM facilities, can be exempted from sentinel health surveillance at the Attending Veterinarian’s discretion.

      4. Sentinel animals are procured from an approved commercial vendor’s SPF/VAF colony.

      5. Mouse sentinels will be tested before distribution throughout the facilities and housed in isolation either in micro-isolator units or separate room until test results are confirmed negative. Feces and fur swabs will be collected from 10 animals when they arrive, samples pooled and sent to the commercial laboratory for PCR evaluation for Mouse Prevalent PRIA panel (PCR).

      6. Immunologically mature (6-8 weeks of age) rodents of like species are placed in each rodent room for an exposure interval of at least10 weeks. Animals are euthanized at the below specified times or transferred to another protocol such as the training protocol if appropriate.

      7. Sentinel animals are housed in pairs in the same manner as the population, receive the same bedding, food and water. One to two sentinel cages are placed per rack for double sided racks depending on the density of occupancy of the particular racks. In rooms with low cage density more racks can be monitored by one sentinel cage. However, each sentinel cage can monitor no more than 70 cages, either IVC (individually ventilated cages) or microisolator cages

      8. Sentinel cages are located on the farthest bottom right slot on individually ventilated racks. In the vivarium in building 71, there will be at least one cage per room with microisolator caging depending on actual cage count. The sentinel cage will be placed either in an empty cube or in the cube with the lowest cage count if all cubes in the room are occupied.

      9. Once sentinels are placed, each sentinel cage is labeled with a cage card containing the following information:

        1. Sentinel’s location in the facility, i.e. room, rack(s), rack side (A – representing lower numbers, B- representing side with higher numbers)

        2. Animal information, i.e. date of birth (DOB), date of placement (DOP), sex, source

        3. PI and IACUC protocol#.

      10. Each sentinel cage/sample is identified by a unique code containing facility affiliation (i.e. COM = COM Building 71, 35A = Building 35A, J17 = Jupiter facility MC17, J19 = Jupiter facility MC19), room number, cube number, rack number(s) and rack side (A or B) if applicable. For example COM412 denotes

        College of Medicine facility building 71, room 412 or 35A/110/7/B denotes building 35A facility, room 110, rack 7 and rack side B.

      11. If sentinels are relocated to another room/rack/rack side with the group of animals they represent, the new location/information is recorded on the cage card so that the sentinel can be traced to all its previous locations.

      12. Maintain and sample a minimum of two sentinel mice or rats per sentinel cage. More sentinels can be housed in the same cage to serve as backups.

      13. At every bedding change, contaminate the sentinel cage with 2-5ml dirty bedding from every monitored cage or more if a small number of cages is monitored.

      14. Colony representative animals can be used to characterize the microbial status of rodent production colonies at the discretion of the veterinarian. Animal(s) will be selected with the help of the PI to assure testing immunologically mature animals that are representative of the health history of their particular colony. Either non- lethal sample techniques will be employed or animals chosen that are not essential for a research study or breeding.

      15. Any unexpected health concern of a sentinel animal has to be recorded including details regarding signs observed, diagnosis, treatment and outcome of case if applicable. Whenever a sentinel is found dead a necropsy has to be performed. If either mice or rats in a sentinel cage die and a cause for death cannot be established a follow up investigation, e.g. testing of a corresponding colony representative, has to be initiated by the veterinary staff. The Attending Veterinarian has to be informed about any health concerns or deaths.

    2. Sample collection

      1. The Attending Veterinarian is responsible for the testing of all sentinels or colony animals assisted by animal care staff trained for the procedures. The findings concerning each sentinel evaluation are recorded and reported to the Attending Veterinarian for interpretation and/or resolution as needed.

      2. Tests are performed and samples for submission to Charles River Laboratories collected either on a quarterly interval for mice or every 4 months for rats.

      3. Sentinels are tested twice a year. One sentinel animal per cage is undergoing the testing described as follows:

        1. Testing for endoparasites is accomplished by fecal flotation. At least one – preferably fresh – pellet is collected per animal. Up to 10 fecal pellets can be collected per fecal flotation from sentinel cages from one housing room.

        2. One sentinel animal per cage is euthanized and blood collected via cardiac stick. The serum or dried blood spot test is submitted for serology for various panels (see table 1)

        3. A gross necropsy will be performed, the cecum evaluated for endoparasites, esp. pinworm as well as histological and microbiological samples collected of lesions when present.

        4. Evaluation for fur mites and pinworm by cellophane tape test taken at the perineum and abdomen. This is an optional test and needs to be performed when clinical or other indications for ectoparasites or pinworm are present.

        5. Mesenteric lymph nodes are collected from all sentinels during the fourth quarter of the calendar year coinciding with the assessment plus serology panel. Mesenteric lymph nodes are pooled and submitted for parvovirus PCR.

        6. The second sentinel per cage is continued to be housed as described above until all test results have been evaluated. If all results are negative this sentinel will either be euthanized without further testing or transferred to the respective training protocol or any other appropriate protocol as determined by the Attending Veterinarian. If any of the results are positive for murine pathogens the responsible veterinarian will determine the further testing approach.

        7. Mesenteric lymph nodes are collected from the second sentinel and frozen at -20°C but only after the first sentinel test result turned out to be equivocal or positive. These specimens are stored until respective results are received and discarded when the serology results are determined to be negative for all pathogens tested.

        8. Mesenteric lymph nodes are collected and frozen at -20° if the backup sentinel is missing from the cage (i.e. removed for a health concern or unexpected death) until respective results are received and discarded when the serology results are determined to be negative for all pathogens tested.

      4. Serum or dried blood spot for testing is sent to Charles River Laboratory to include different serology profiles, which is either prevalent, tracking or assessment plus panel.

      5. Samples from colony animals are collected twice a year from mice and once a year from rats (see table 1).

      6. Only samples from animals of the same health status will be pooled.

  4. Test Results and Record Maintenance

    1. All positive results of in-house tests have to be confirmed by the veterinarian.

    2. Test results received from Charles River have to be reviewed by the Attending Veterinarian and appropriate follow up steps implemented if results are equivocal or positive for tested pathogens.

    3. Additional health evaluations may be conducted upon request from the research staff or at the discretion of the Attending Veterinarian.

    4. All results, written reports and evaluations as a result of these procedures are maintained by the responsible care technician as a hard copy and electronically organized by year and location. Test results will be archived if older than 3 years.

The University...at a Glance

Some universities measure greatness in decades and centuries. Florida Atlantic University measures it with every student who earns a degree, every researcher who makes a discovery and every community that is transformed.

At FAU's dedication ceremony in 1964, President Lyndon B. Johnson challenged the University's pioneers: "It is time now...for a new, adventurous, imaginative, courageous breakthrough for a new revolution in education in America."

With those words, FAU opened its doors as the first public university in southeast Florida and the first in America designed for upper division students only. Since day one, FAU has pushed the bounds of higher education. Now, 50 years later, the University serves more than 30,000 freshmen, transfers and graduate students at sites throughout its six-county service region in southeast Florida.

People from every walk of life find a place at FAU. Students choose from more than 170 degree programs, faculty researchers utilize more than 40 research centers and the community engages hundreds of cultural and educational events every year.
