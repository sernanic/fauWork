SCOPE:  This SOP is applicable to all Comparative Medicine personnel involved in carcass disposal procedures.
SOP OWNER:  Facility Manager (FM)
PURPOSE:   To outline the procedures for carcass disposal in the vivaria at FAU.
LOCATION:  All Vivaria laboratories

  1. References

    1. The Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals, page 45, 73 and 77

    2. EH&S Biological Waste Program

  2. Responsibilities

    1. Lab Animal Technician

      1. Prepare for disposal of FAU EH&S animal pick-up by making sure all animals are easily accessible in designated refrigerators.

      2. Clean fluid leakage from carcass disposal areas and disinfect areas of fluid leakage.

      3. Contact EH&S for animal pick-up as needed.

    2. Facility Manager (FM)

      1. Ensure compliance to procedures as outlined in this SOP.

      2. Ensure appropriate training is provided to personnel.

      3. Inform IACU of live animals found in carcass disposal.

    3. Training Coordinator

      1. Schedule investigation of animal welfare issue with appropriate persons.

      2. Schedule training at frequency to maintain competency to SOP.

      3. Ensure appropriate training is provided to personnel.

    4. Director/Veterinarian

      1. Ensure appropriate training is provided to personnel and necessary disposal resources are available via EH&S or biohazard disposal vendor.

  3. Materials

    1. Carcass disposal bag(s)

    2. Carcass Labels

    3. Freezer and/or refrigerator

  4. Safety

    1. Use of universal precautions is necessary when handling carcass disposal bags.

    2. Fluid leakage from carcass bags must be cleaned and then the surface must be sanitized.

  5. Procedure

    1. Verify death. If the animal was euthanized, this needs to be ensured via a secondary method (see IACUC Policy 10.4.11: Euthanasia Methods).

    2. Wearing gloves, place the carcass in a carcass bag

    3. Fill out carcass sticker information (profile numbers are posted by each euthanasia station) roll the top of the bag down, and adhere carcass sticker in a way that seals the bag.

    4. Animal carcasses are stored in appropriate carcass storage areas (vivarium 35A&B: building 35A/room 105, vivarium 71: dirty hallway, vivarium MC17&MC19: MC17 hallway).

    5. If animal is found dead, observe for any physical abnormalities such as fight wounds or misaligned teeth. Place the carcass in a refrigerator (not a freezer) for 24 hours and notify lab personnel via email so they have a chance to collect tissues if needed.

    6. Contact the FM or ehs@fau.edu to request a carcass pickup once the freezer starts getting full.

    7. The FM is copied on written communications regarding carcass pick-ups.

    8. Any animals found alive or outside of carcass disposal bags must be reported to the FM who will initiate the proper follow-up steps including informing the Attending Veterinarian.

  6. Record Keeping

    1. If animals are found alive in carcass disposal refrigerators, the Facility Manger will find the open bag in the freezer to pinpoint the identity of the mouse via the carcass label.

    2. All refrigerators are to be labeled with proper signage / laboratory labels

      1. No Food or Drink Storage – For laboratory use only

      2. Biohazardous Waste

The University...at a Glance

Some universities measure greatness in decades and centuries. Florida Atlantic University measures it with every student who earns a degree, every researcher who makes a discovery and every community that is transformed.

At FAU's dedication ceremony in 1964, President Lyndon B. Johnson challenged the University's pioneers: "It is time now...for a new, adventurous, imaginative, courageous breakthrough for a new revolution in education in America."

With those words, FAU opened its doors as the first public university in southeast Florida and the first in America designed for upper division students only. Since day one, FAU has pushed the bounds of higher education. Now, 50 years later, the University serves more than 30,000 freshmen, transfers and graduate students at sites throughout its six-county service region in southeast Florida.

People from every walk of life find a place at FAU. Students choose from more than 170 degree programs, faculty researchers utilize more than 40 research centers and the community engages hundreds of cultural and educational events every year.
