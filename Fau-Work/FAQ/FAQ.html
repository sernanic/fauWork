Introduction

Technology transfer is a term used to describe a formal transfer of rights to use and commercialize new discoveries and innovations resulting from scientific research to another party.


The Office of Technology Development manages intellectual property (IP) created by faculty and students at FAU. It provides impartial consultation on various legal protection and commercialization strategies, and collaborates with outside attorneys and consultants to offer additional resources. The office also works closely with the Office of Sponsored Programs to structure the IP and commercial provisions of sponsored research agreements.

You should contact the Office of Technology Development during your early research activities to be aware of the options that will best leverage the commercial potential of your technology. The Office of Technology Development will assist you with questions related to marketability, funding sources, commercial partners, patenting and other legal protection methods, and FAU’s policies and procedures.

Technology is typically transferred through a license agreement in which FAU grants rights in a technology to a third party for a set period of time, often limited to a particular field of use and region of the world. The licensee (the third party licensing the technology) may be an established company or a new business startup. Licenses typically include terms that require the licensee to meet certain performance requirements and pay royalties to FAU.

The process of obtaining legal protection for a technology and finding the right licensing partner may take months or even years to complete. The amount time will depend on factors such as the development stage of the technology, the market for the technology, competing technologies, and the resources available to the inventors and licensees.

Invention Disclosures and Works Disclosures

A disclosure is the written notice to the Office of Technology Development that begins the formal technology transfer process. It is a confidential document that describes your invention/work in detail so that options for legal protection and commercialization can be evaluated and pursued. When a patentable invention is disclosed, the invention disclosure form is used. When a copyrightable work is disclosed, the works disclosure form is used.

Typically an invention/work is disclosed when a researcher feels he or she has discovered something unique with possible commercial value. Disclosure to the Office of Technology Development should occur well before any publication, presentation, or any other public communication of the invention/work. If an invention is publicly disclosed prior to the filing of a patent application, the invention cannot be patented outside the United States (U.S. protection may still be available). The Office of Technology Development should be informed of any prior or imminent presentations, lectures, posters, abstracts, proposals, publications, or other public communications of the invention/work in order to determine an appropriate strategy for legal protection.

The Invention Disclosure and Works Disclosure forms are available to download from the Office of Technology Development webpage under Forms and Policies. Completed disclosure forms should be submitted via email to techdevelop@fau.edu.

Inventorship, Authorship, and Ownership

Individuals should be listed as inventors on an invention disclosure form if they meet the legal definition of “inventor”. An inventor is legally defined as one who makes an intellectual contribution (either independently or jointly) to the conception and/or development of the invention. Individuals should be listed as authors on a works disclosure form if they meet the legal definition of “author”. An author is legally defined as the creator (either independently or jointly) of the original expression in a work.

IAll inventors/authors should be listed on an invention/works disclosure form, even if they are not affiliated with FAU. The Office of Technology Development will contact the technology transfer office of the non-FAU institution or company to discuss an appropriate management plan for the invention/work.

As a general rule, FAU owns inventions/works created by FAU employees, including but not limited to faculty and graduate students. Depending on the specific circumstances surrounding the creation of an invention/work, FAU may choose to either assert or waive its ownership interest. The complete FAU Intellectual Property Policy is available for review on the Office of Technology Development webpage under Forms and Policies .

If an invention was created in the process of research funded by the government, the government retains certain rights in the invention. These rights are codified in the Bayh-Dole Act of 1980 (35 USC §200-212). The act allows universities and other non-profit institutions to have ownership rights to discoveries resulting from federally-funded research, provided certain obligations are met. These obligations include making efforts to protect and commercialize the discoveries, submitting progress reports to the funding agency, substantial manufacture in the U.S., and sharing any resulting revenues with the inventors.

Intellectual Property

Intellectual property (IP) refers to creations of the mind such as inventions, literary and artistic works, symbols, names, images, and designs. IP is protected through patents, copyrights, trademarks and trade secrets.

A patent is a legal document that establishes certain rights in an invention, including limited rights to exclude others from making, using, selling, or importing the invention. A patent has two major components—a technical description of the invention and how to practice it (the “specification”), and the legal definition of the invention (the “claims”)

Patentable subject matter includes processes, machines, compositions of matter, articles of manufacture, and in certain cases computer programs and methods.

The Office of Technology Development engages outside legal counsel to draft and file patent applications. It takes approximately 24 months from the date of filing for a patent examiner from the U.S. Patent &Trademark Office (USPTO) to respond to an application. The response will either approve the application or reject it in the form of an Office Action. An application may receive an Office Action if the patent examiner argues that the application’s claims are not patentable over the “prior art” (any patents or other documents that were publicly disclosed before the subject application was filed). To overcome the examiner’s argument, outside counsel may file a written response to the Office Action amending the application’s claims and/or arguing why the patent examiner’s position is incorrect. This process is referred to as patent prosecution. Once the application is resolved, the patent examiner issues a Notice of Allowance that states that the USPTO has agreed to issue a patent.

Publishing or presenting research results may prevent patent protection of an invention. Inventions should be disclosed to the Office of Technology Development before they are publically disclosed outside of FAU. A public disclosure includes papers, presentations, posters, abstracts, proposals, and any other public communication that describes the invention in detail. Once an invention is publicly disclosed there is only a limited window of time to file a patent application in the United States, and international patent protection is no longer available.

The USPTO keeps patent applications confidential until they are published, approximately 18 months after the initial filing date. After that time, both the application and the USPTO’s prosecution materials related to the application are publicly available.

A provisional patent application is an application that can be filed with the USPTO that establishes the effective filing date (priority date) of a patent application. The provisional application has a term of only twelve months, and is not examined by the USPTO. At the end of the twelve-month period, the applicant must elect to either abandon the filing or convert the provisional application to a non-provisional (utility) patent application that is formally examined by the USPTO.

Foreign patent protection is subject to the laws of each individual country, but the process is somewhat similar to the patent prosecution system in the United States. If an inventor is interested in pursuing patent protection for an invention outside of the United States, the Office of Technology Development will usually direct outside patent counsel to file a Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) application. The Patent Cooperation Treaty is an international patent law treaty that provides a unified procedure for filing patent applications to protect inventions in each of its member countries. Most countries are members of the Patent Cooperation Treaty and accept PCT patent applications.

The average pendency for a patent application from filing to issuance in the United States is 24 months. Once a patent is issued, it is generally enforceable for 20 years from the date of filing.

Copyright is a form of protection provided by the laws of the United States to the authors of “original works of authorship.” This includes literary, dramatic, musical, artistic, and certain other intellectual works as well as computer software. This protection is available to both published and unpublished works. The Copyright Act generally gives the owner of copyright the exclusive right to conduct and authorize various acts, including reproduction, public performance and making derivative works.

The Office of Technology Development can file a copyright registration, but in most cases it is unnecessary. Copyright protection is automatically secured when an original work of authorship is fixed into a tangible medium of expression such as a book, painting, song, video, or software code. The only benefits of registering a copyright are that it creates a public record of ownership and allows the owner to sue for copyright infringement and collect statutory damages.

A trademark or service mark includes any word, name, symbol, device, or combination thereof, that is used in commerce to identify and distinguish the source of goods or services of one manufacturer or seller from another.

The Office of Technology Development can file a trademark or service mark registration, but in most cases it is unnecessary. Trademarks and service marks become protected as soon as they are used in commerce. The only benefit of registering a trademark or service mark is that it creates a legal presumption that the registrant is entitled to use the trademark throughout the United States for the goods or services for which the trademark or service mark is registered.

Marketing, Licensing, and Commercialization

The Office of Technology Development uses a variety of resources and strategies to market inventions/works. In-depth market research assists in identifying market trends, competing technologies, and potential licensees. Posting inventions/works on the Office of Technology Development webpage and other portals gives inventions/works more exposure. Attending conferences and events generates interest among industry contacts.

In many cases, licensees are contacts already known by the inventors/authors. These contacts are acquired through sources such as research, consulting, and alumni. Licensees are also identified through relationships cultivated by the Office of Technology Development. The office works to broaden these relationships through contacts obtained from market research inquiries and industry events.

It can take months or even years to identify a licensee, depending on the attractiveness of the invention/work, its stage of development, competition, and the size of the market. Most university inventions/works tend to be in the early stages of development and require substantial commercialization investment, which makes it difficult to immediately attract a licensee.

Many licensees require the assistance of the inventor/author to facilitate their commercialization efforts. This can range from occasional informal communication to a formal consulting relationship or sponsored research. Working with a new business startup may require a more significant time commitment, depending on the inventor’s/author’s role with the company. Acting as consultant and/or involvement with a startup is governed by FAU’s conflict of interest policies, which can be found on the Office of Research Integrity webpage under Financial Conflict of Interest.

Per FAU’s Intellectual Property Policy, IP revenue such as license fees, royalties, and equity—minus any unreimbursed legal expenses—is split among inventors/authors, colleges, departments, and the FAU Research Corporation. The full policy is available for review on the Office of Technology Development webpage under Forms and Policies .
